Get the Look: Italian Cypress Style in the Columbia Basin

Love the tall, sculptural look of Italian cypress? Here’s how to achieve it in the Columbia Basin.

By Civilization Tri-Cities

[Month Year]

Few landscape elements create a stronger architectural statement than Italian cypress. Tall, narrow, and unmistakably vertical, they’ve long been associated with Mediterranean landscapes—lining driveways, framing entryways, and punctuating horizons with a kind of formal simplicity.

It’s a look that translates surprisingly well to the Tri-Cities. The region’s wide skies, strong light, and open lots make vertical elements feel especially pronounced. But while the aesthetic fits, the plant itself doesn’t always.

Italian cypress can struggle in the Columbia Basin’s colder winters and persistent wind. The good news is that you don’t need the exact plant to achieve the same effect.

Get the Look

You can recreate the tall, sculptural presence of Italian cypress using trees that are better suited to the Columbia Basin.

Taylor Juniper

The closest match to Italian cypress, Taylor juniper offers the same narrow, upright form with far better performance in cold, dry climates. It grows tall and columnar, maintaining that distinctive vertical silhouette without requiring constant maintenance or protection.

Other Options

  • Skyrocket Juniper – extremely narrow and fast-growing, closest in silhouette
  • Blue Arrow Juniper – similar shape with a cooler blue-gray tone
  • Spartan Juniper – slightly wider, better suited for hedges or screening

How to Use the Look

The impact of Italian cypress comes from repetition and placement as much as the trees themselves. Planted in rows or pairs, they create rhythm and symmetry—often used to frame driveways, define boundaries, or anchor entry points.

In the Tri-Cities, these forms work especially well against stucco, stone, or modern siding, where their vertical lines contrast with low, horizontal architecture and open landscapes.
